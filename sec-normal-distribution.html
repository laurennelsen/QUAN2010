    Section 7.1 The Normal Distribution

    Now we‚Äôre going to investigate each of these three distributions, starting with the normal distribution.
    üîó

    Subsection 7.1.1 Introduction to the Normal Probability Distribution:

    Exercise 7.1.1. Continuous Probability Distribution Matching Problem.

      Match each probability distribution with the situation in which it is useful.
      üîó
    • Normal Distribution
    • Useful when data tends to fall in the center and extreme values are rare
      üîó
    • exponential distribution
    • Useful when low values for data occur often and high values are rare
      üîó
    • uniform distribution
    • Useful when data of all values have an equal chance of occurring
      üîó
    üîó
    The mathematical expression that describes the shape of the normal probability distribution is called the normal probability density function:
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-0.5[(x-\mu)/\sigma]^2} \end{equation*}
    Despite the complexity of this function, there are only two parameters that completely deter- mine the shape of the distribution: the mean, \(\mu\text{,}\) and the standard deviation, \(\sigma\text{.}\) Let‚Äôs take a closer look at how they impact the distribution. (Don‚Äôt worry---we won‚Äôt be using this formula to compute probabilities. We have other tools!)
    üîó

    Question 7.1.2.

    How does changing the standard deviation (\(\sigma\)) and the mean (\(\mu\)) change the curve?
    üîó
    Answer.
    • \(\sigma:\) controls the ‚Äúspread‚Äù of the curve. Larger \(\sigma\) results in a flatter, more spread out curve
      üîó
      üîó
    • \(\mu:\) controls the location of the center. Larger \(\mu\) shifts the ‚Äúhill‚Äù of the curve to the right
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 7.1.2 Characteristics of the Normal Probability Distribution:

    1. The distribution is bell-shaped and symmetric around the mean.
      üîó
      üîó
    2. Because the shape is symmetrical, the mean and median are equal and located at the center of the distribution.
      üîó
      üîó
    3. Random variables around the mean, where the curve is the tallest have a higher probability of occurring than values toward the end of the distribution.
      üîó
      üîó
    4. The total area under the curve is \(1\text{.}\) Since the distribution is symmetric, the area to the left of the mean equals \(0.5\) as does the area to the right of the mean
      üîó
      üîó
    5. No matter how far in either direction the distribution curve extends, it never touches the horizontal axis -- but it gets increasingly closer.
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 7.1.4 The Standard Normal Distribution:

    Recall that the \(z\)-score of a value, \(x\text{,}\) describes the number of standard deviations that the value \(x\) is from the mean of its distribution:
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \end{equation*}

    Definition 7.1.8.

    When the original random variable, \(x\text{,}\) follows a normal distribution, \(z\)-scores also follow a normal distribution with \(\mu=0\text{,}\) \(\sigma=1\text{.}\) It is called the standard normal distribution.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 7.1.5 Calculating Probabilities for Normal Distributions:

    The standard normal distribution allows us to calculate probabilities for any normal distribution since we can standardize it with \(z\)-scores. The tables at this link (Standard Normal Table) provide you with the cumulative area to the LEFT of the \(z\)-score.
    üîó
    We can also use Excel to calculate the cumulative area to the left of the \(z\)-score by typing
    \begin{equation*} =NORM.S.DIST(x,TRUE) \end{equation*}
    üîó
    (Note: We can type ‚Äú1‚Äù instead of ‚ÄúTRUE‚Äù in this formula in Excel.)
    üîó

    Example 7.1.10.

    Find the probability \(P(Z\leq -1.3)\) for a standard normal distribution
    üîó
    Answer.
    In Excel:
    \begin{equation*} =NORM.S.DIST(-1.3,1)\approx 0.0968 \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    So how do we use the table or the Excel formula to find any probability for a normal distribution?
    üîó
    There are 3 basic types of problems:
    1. To the left of any \(z\)-score: Look up the \(z\)-score in the table (or calculate it in Excel) and use the area given.
      üîó
      üîó
    2. To the right of any \(z\)-score: Look up the \(z\)-score in the table (or calculate it in Excel) subtract the area from 1.
      üîó
      üîó
    3. Between any two \(z\)-scores: Look up both \(z\)-scores in the table (or calculate them in Excel) and subtract the smaller area from the larger area.
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 7.1.6 Calculating Normal Distribution Values in Excel:

    We already saw an Excel command that will allow us to find a probability associated with a standard normal distribution. There is also an Excel command that allows us to find a probability associated with a general normal distribution, and in this case, we will need to specify the mean and standard deviation of the distribution. (These first two commands both apply to situations where the goal is to compute a probability associated with a data value.)
    • \(NORM.DIST(x,\text{mean},\text{standard deviation},\text{cumulative})\text{:}\) applies to problems involving a normal distribution with a specified mean and standard deviation
      üîó
      üîó
    • \(NORM.S.DIST(x,\text{cumulative})\text{:}\) applies to problems involving the standard normal distribution (\(\mu=0\text{ and }\sigma=1\))
      üîó
      üîó
    (As we‚Äôve seen before, cumulative\(=TRUE\text{ or }FALSE\text{.}\))
    üîó
