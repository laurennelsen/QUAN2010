    Section 7.1 The Normal Distribution

    The mathematical expression that describes the shape of the normal probability distribution is called the normal probability density function:
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-0.5[(x-\mu)/\sigma]^2} \end{equation*}
    Despite the complexity of this function, there are only two parameters that completely deter- mine the shape of the distribution: the mean, \(\mu\text{,}\) and the standard deviation, \(\sigma\text{.}\) Let’s take a closer look at how they impact the distribution. (Don’t worry---we won’t be using this formula to compute probabilities. We have other tools!)

    Question 7.1.1.

    How does changing the standard deviation (\(\sigma\)) and the mean (\(\mu\)) change the curve?
    Answer.
    • \(\sigma:\) controls the “spread” of the curve. Larger \(\sigma\) results in a flatter, more spread out curve
    • \(\mu:\) controls the location of the center. Larger \(\mu\) shifts the “hill” of the curve to the right

    Subsection 7.1.1 Characteristics of the Normal Probability Distribution:

    1. The distribution is bell-shaped and symmetric around the mean.
    2. Because the shape is symmetrical, the mean and median are equal and located at the center of the distribution.
    3. Random variables around the mean, where the curve is the tallest have a higher probability of occurring than values toward the end of the distribution.
    4. The total area under the curve is \(1\text{.}\) Since the distribution is symmetric, the area to the left of the mean equals \(0.5\) as does the area to the right of the mean
    5. No matter how far in either direction the distribution curve extends, it never touches the horizontal axis -- but it gets increasingly closer.

    Recall that the \(z\)-score of a value, \(x\text{,}\) describes the number of standard deviations that the value \(x\) is from the mean of its distribution:
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \end{equation*}

    Exercise 7.1.6.

    A coffee shop wants to understand the distribution of amounts that their customers are spending per order. They find that the average amount a customer spends per order is \(\$ 7.50\text{,}\) and the standard deviation is \(\$1.25\text{.}\) If we assume that the amounts spent per order are normally distributed, and Rey comes in and spends \(\$10.00\text{,}\) what is the \(z\)-score for Rey’s order?
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{10.00-7.50}{1.25} = 2 \end{equation*}

    Definition 7.1.7.

    When the original random variable, \(x\text{,}\) follows a normal distribution, \(z\)-scores also follow a normal distribution with \(\mu=0\text{,}\) \(\sigma=1\text{.}\) It is called the standard normal distribution.

    Subsection 7.1.4 Calculating Probabilities for Normal Distributions:

    The standard normal distribution allows us to calculate probabilities for any normal distribution since we can standardize it with \(z\)-scores. The tables at this link (Standard Normal Table
     1 
    www.math.arizona.edu/~rsims/ma464/standardnormaltable.pdf
    ) provide you with the cumulative area to the LEFT of the \(z\)-score.
    We can also use Excel to calculate the cumulative area to the left of the \(z\)-score by typing
    \begin{equation*} =NORM.S.DIST(x,TRUE) \end{equation*}
    (Note: We can type “1” instead of “TRUE” in this formula in Excel.)

    Example 7.1.9.

    Find the probability \(P(Z\leq -1.3)\) for a standard normal distribution
    Answer.
    In Excel: \(=NORM.S.DIST(-1.3,TRUE)\approx 0.0968 \)
    So how do we use the table or the Excel formula to find any probability for a normal distribution?
    There are 3 basic types of problems:
    1. To the left of any \(z\)-score: Look up the \(z\)-score in the table (or calculate it in Excel) and use the area given.
    2. To the right of any \(z\)-score: Look up the \(z\)-score in the table (or calculate it in Excel) subtract the area from 1.
    3. Between any two \(z\)-scores: Look up both \(z\)-scores in the table (or calculate them in Excel) and subtract the smaller area from the larger area.

    Example 7.1.10.

    Find the following probabilities for a standard normal distribution:
    (a)
    \(P(Z\lt-0.5)\)
    Answer.
    In Excel: \(=NORM.S.DIST(-0.5,TRUE)\approx 0.3085 \)
    (b)
    \(P(Z\gt 2.1)\)
    Answer.
    In Excel: \(=1-NORM.S.DIST(2.1,TRUE)\approx 0.0179 \)
    (c)
    \(P(0.2\leq Z\leq 2.7)\)
    Answer.
    In Excel: \(=NORM.S.DIST(2.7,TRUE)-NORM.S.DIST(0.2,TRUE)\approx 0.4173 \)
    We already saw an Excel command that will allow us to find a probability associated with a standard normal distribution. There is also an Excel command that allows us to find a probability associated with a general normal distribution, and in this case, we will need to specify the mean and standard deviation of the distribution. (These first two commands both apply to situations where the goal is to compute a probability associated with a data value.)
    • \(NORM.DIST(x,\text{mean},\text{standard deviation},\text{cumulative})\text{:}\) applies to problems involving a normal distribution with a specified mean and standard deviation
    • \(NORM.S.DIST(x,\text{cumulative})\text{:}\) applies to problems involving the standard normal distribution (\(\mu=0\text{ and }\sigma=1\))
    (As we’ve seen before, cumulative\(=TRUE\text{ or }FALSE\text{.}\))

    Subsection 7.1.5 Finding \(Z\) or \(X\) if we have information about a probability:

    What if we want to know the specific \(X\) that satisfies a given probability? Then we can work backward using the tables (Standard Normal Table
     2 
    www.math.arizona.edu/~rsims/ma464/standardnormaltable.pdf
    ) and use the \(z\)-score formula in reverse. If the exact probability cannot be found in the table, then we can use the closest values.
    The Excel formulas that apply to this situation are similar to the ones we saw earlier, replacing .DIST with .INV:
    • NORM.INV(probability, mean, standard deviation) applies to normal distributions with a specified mean and standard deviation
    • NORM.S.INV(probability) is used for standard normal distributions

    Exercise 7.1.20.

    (a)
    What value of \(X\) represents the 75th percentile?
    Answer.
    \(NORM.INV(0.75,50,20)\approx 63.49\)
    (b)
    Find the value of \(X\) so that \(40\%\) probability lies below it.
    Answer.
    \(NORM.INV(0.40,50,20)\approx 44.93\)
    (c)
    Find the value of \(X\) that represents the highest \(10\%\) of all values
    Answer.
    \(NORM.DIST(0.90,50,20)\approx 75.63\)

    Exercise 7.1.21.

    Suppose the retirement age of professional athletes in a certain league is normally distributed with mean \(32.27\) years and standard deviation \(3.62\) years. Find the following:
    (a)
    Find the probability that a randomly selected athlete’s retirement age is is at least \(30.15\) years.
    Answer.
    \(P(X\geq 30.15)=0.7209397092241314\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Find the probability that a randomly selected athlete’s retirement age is between \(30.33\) and \(39.65\) years.
    Answer.
    \(P(30.33\lt X\lt 39.65)=0.6832488917348373\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Find \(X^*\) such that the probability that a randomly selected athlete’s retirement age is is at most \(X^*\) years is \(68\%\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    If \(P(X\leq X^*)=68\%\) then \(X=33.96306965279452\) years.
    (d)
    Find \(k\) such that the probability that a randomly selected athlete’s retirement age is between \(32.27-k\) years and \(32.27+k\) years is \(42\%\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    If \(P(32.27-k\lt X\lt 32.27+k)=42\%\) then \(k=2.0032526847915357\) years.
